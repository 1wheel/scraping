Effects of Natural Cyclic Variations on Contaminated Fate and Transport

EPA Grant Number: R828598C700
Subproject: this is subproject number 700 , established and managed by the Center Director under grant R828598
(EPA does not fund or establish subprojects; EPA awards and manages the overall grant for this center).
Center: HSRC (2001) - South and Southwest HSRC
Center Director: Reible, Danny D.
Title: Effects of Natural Cyclic Variations on Contaminated Fate and Transport
Investigators: Tomson, Mason B. , Kan, Amy T.
Institution: Rice University
Current Institution: Rice University , Rice University
EPA Project Officer: Lasat, Mitch
Project Period: September 1, 1998 through August 31, 2001
Project Amount: Refer to main center abstract for funding details.
RFA: Gulf Coast Hazardous Substance Research Center (Lamar University) (1996) RFA Text |  Recipients Lists
Research Category: Hazardous Waste/Remediation , Targeted Research

Objective:

Nearly all natural processes are either cyclic or episodic, yet laboratory efforts to simulate fate and transport are almost always steady state column or batch experiments. If the mechanisms of contaminant fate are fundamentally linear (not necessarily first order) and reversible, then there are well established mathematical procedures to model the impact of cyclic or episodic (e.g., a single or stepped spike, etc., events) variations. Unfortunately, many natural processes of importance to contaminant fate are non‑reversible as they occur in lakes, streams, and estuaries. It will be shown that these non‑reversible processes are expected to have previously unexpected and profound impacts on the fate of common contaminants in natural systems of particular importance to the Gulf Coast HSRC regions.

Commonly recognized processes that are known to change over scales of space and time of interest to modeling include: temperature, salinity, pH, redox potential, and oxygen tension. The overall objective of this research will be to develop model procedures and equations which can be used to predict the effects of changes in natural environmental variables on slow, or resistant, desorption and long‑term clean‑up.

Approach:

In recent years, numerous researchers have observed that contaminant desorption processes are often not reversible. This has been observed for both inorganic and organic compounds. In most cases the desorption process has been found to be much slower than the adsorption reaction. Explanations and models advanced to explain these observations have included intra‑particle diffusion limitations, bulk linear driving‑force limitations, simply slow kinetics, experimental artifacts, unaccounted‑for chemical reactions, etc. The general consensus is that at least practical adsorption and desorption of hydrophobic organic contaminants can not be described as linear reversible processes. Desorption from this resistant fraction often accounts for greater than half of the clean‑up effort with little, or no, improvement in environmental quality.

Expected Results:

The effect of changes in T, TDS, pH, and Eh on adsorption and desorption of hydrophobic organic compounds from the low‑level resistant compartment will first be modeled in the laboratory using the highly reproducible experimental methods developed by the PIs at Rice University with HSRC S&SW support. Results of these design and modeling efforts will be useable by regulators, industry personnel in the GCHSRC regions, and the Army Corp of Engineers to more efficiently manage environmental contamination and reduce risk. If successful, greater than 50% of the cost of remediation and management will be eliminated with no real increase in human or environmental risk.
