Development of Sensitive Biomarkers Based on Chemically Induced Changes in Expressions of Oncogenes

EPA Grant Number: R825433C071
Subproject: this is subproject number 071 , established and managed by the Center Director under grant R825433
(EPA does not fund or establish subprojects; EPA awards and manages the overall grant for this center).
Center: EERC - Center for Ecological Health Research (Cal Davis)
Center Director: Rolston, Dennis E.
Title: Development of Sensitive Biomarkers Based on Chemically Induced Changes in Expressions of Oncogenes
Investigators: Matsumura, Fumio , Hinton, David E.
Institution: University of California - Davis
EPA Project Officer: Levinson, Barbara
Project Period: June 30, 1995 through June 30, 1998
Project Amount: Refer to main center abstract for funding details.
RFA: Exploratory Environmental Research Centers (1992) RFA Text |  Recipients Lists
Research Category: Targeted Research

Objective:

This project seeks to focus on biochemical and molecular biological marker technologies to detect subtle effects of multiple stresses on organisms. Such "biomarkers" should be useful first as a research tool to understand which combinations of stresses act synergistically, additively or antagonistically in the given organism which occupies an important ecological position in various Center research areas. Second, after the baseline data are accumulated, the methods should offer the possibility of using them in "real world" situations to help assess the health of the given ecosystem.

Approach:

In the past several years the investigators' approach has been highly successful in developing and providing several markers: (1) Pan-ras antibody to detect the increase in expression of ras-oncogenes in collaboration with (2) 125-Iodine epidermal growth factor (EGF) binding assay which has been utilized to assess dioxin-type effects in incineration emission, (3) Antibodies to heat shock protein 70 , (4) Glucose transporters, and (5) those for metallothioneins in plants. In all cases the investigators developed the marker technologies and the collaborative scientists are applying such technologies to "real world" problems, or as new research tools to understand the basics of stress expression in the organisms of their studies. In addition the investigators efforts to find good examples of their applicability are focusing the effects of multiple stresses in rice plants, brine shrimp, fathead minnows and bullfrog tadpoles, Rana catesbeina, being used as a surrogate species for Rana muscosa, mountain yellow-legged frogs which are disappearing from the Sierra Nevada.

Expected Results:

The investigators expect to continue to study basic patterns of stressor interactions using antibodies to HSP70, metallothioneins, glucose transporters and glucose regulated proteins in brine shrimp and fathead minnows. Stresses studied will be: high and low temperature, salinity, pH, and lack of oxygen, in combination with exposure to toxic chemicals (lindane, DDT, toxaphene, TCDD, PCB, o.p-DDT, chlordane).

Investigators also expect to develop new classes of markers, one for protein kinase C and the other for c-Neu kinase, both of which are important protein kinases regulating growth factor signaling processes as possible markers. The latter is related to estrogenic actions of xenobiotics.
